      Artificial Intelligence

      A 75-year story in 12 scenes.

      From Turing’s notebook to Gemini 3 — in one lifetime.

      scroll ↓

      Chapter 01 · 1940s – 1955

      The Dream

      Before it had a name.

      In 1950, Alan Turing asked: “Can machines think?” — and invented a test to find out. Seven years earlier, McCulloch & Pitts had already sketched the first mathematical neuron.

      Fun fact

      Turing called it “The Imitation Game” — a parlor game where you guess who’s behind the curtain. Sound familiar?

      Curiosity

      Turing predicted machines would fool humans 30% of the time by the year 2000. He was right — only ~23 years late.

      Chapter 02 · Summer 1956

      The Birth

      10 men, 2 months, 70 years.

      At a summer workshop in Dartmouth College, John McCarthy coins two words that will change the world: “Artificial Intelligence.”

      Fun fact

      The proposal asked for 2 months and 10 people to “solve” AI. We’re still working on it.

      Curiosity

      McCarthy picked the name “AI” partly to distance himself from Norbert Wiener’s cybernetics camp. It was academic politics as much as science.

      Chapter 03 · 1956 – 1974

      The Golden Years

      Symbols, logic, and a therapist.

      Early AI runs on rules. The Logic Theorist proves math theorems. ELIZA (1966) pretends to be a therapist — and everyone falls for it.

      The ELIZA effect

      Weizenbaum’s own secretary asked him to leave the room so she could talk to ELIZA privately. He spent the rest of his life warning us not to trust chatbots.

      Curiosity

      Shakey the Robot (1966–72) could navigate a room. It took 8 hours to cross it.

      > Hello, I am ELIZA.
> How are you feeling today?
_ I feel nervous about AI.
> Why do you feel nervous about AI?
_ Because it's getting smart fast.
> Tell me more about "getting smart fast".

      Chapter 04 · 1974 – 1980

      The First Winter

      The promises froze over.

      The Lighthill Report tears apart AI’s grand claims. Funding evaporates. Research slows to a crawl.

      Moravec’s Paradox

      It’s easy to teach a computer to play chess.
      It’s hard to teach it to tell a cat from a plastic bag.

      Chapter 05 · 1980 – 1987

      Expert Systems

      Rules all the way down.

      MYCIN diagnoses infections. XCON configures computers. Japan bets the farm on the Fifth Generation Project.

      Fun fact

      DEC saved $40 million/year with XCON — the first AI system that actually paid for itself.

      Curiosity

      The LISP Machine was a whole computer built to run one language. Then PCs killed it, and the whole industry with it.

      Chapter 06 · 1987 – 1993

      The Second Winter

      Rinse. Repeat.

      Cheap PCs kill specialized AI hardware. LISP Machines go extinct. The field learns humility — again.

      Chapter 07 · 1993 – 2011

      The Quiet Revolution

      Machine learning learns.

      No hype. Just results. Deep Blue beats Kasparov in chess (1997). Watson wins Jeopardy! (2011). The Netflix Prize, PageRank, spam filters — AI is everywhere, and nobody calls it AI.

      Fun fact

      Kasparov thought IBM was cheating. Deep Blue had made a move so strange it felt human. It was actually a bug — when stuck, Deep Blue picked a random legal move.

      Chapter 08 · 2012 – 2017

      The Awakening

      When neurons woke up.

      AlexNet (2012) drops ImageNet error by 10 points overnight. GPUs + big data + deep nets finally click.

      The bar-napkin moment

      Ian Goodfellow invented GANs in a bar in 2014 — on a dare from colleagues. He went home and coded them that night.

      Move 37

      In 2016, AlphaGo played a move against Lee Sedol so alien that commentators said “a human would never do that.” AlphaGo Zero later learned Go from nothing and beat that version 100–0.

      Chapter 09 · 2017 – 2022

      The Transformer Era

      “Attention is all you need.”

      A 2017 Google paper quietly rewrites everything. GPT-1, 2, 3. Suddenly, scale becomes a feature. DALL·E and Stable Diffusion let AI make pictures.

      Fun fact

      In 2018, an AI-generated portrait — “Edmond de Belamy” — sold at Christie’s for $432,500. It was signed with the loss function that made it.

      Chapter 10 · Nov 2022 – 2024

      The ChatGPT Moment

      The week everyone noticed.

      100,000,000
      users in 2 months. Fastest-growing app in history.
      TikTok took 9 months. Instagram took 2½ years.
      Curiosity

      GPT-3.5-level inference got 280× cheaper between Nov 2022 and Oct 2024.

      Curiosity

      Training GPT-4 cost $41–71M. Training Gemini: $30–191M. Electricity bills matter.

      Chapter 11 · 2025 – 2026

      Today — State of the Art

      Reasoning, agents, and chip designers.

      Reasoning

      GPT-5, Claude Opus 4.6, Gemini 3 Pro — models that think before they answer.

      94.3% on GPQA Diamond

      Agents

      Multi-step autonomous work. Grok 4 runs three specialists — Harper, Benjamin, Lucas — who debate before replying.

      83% on GDPval knowledge-work bench

      Self-improving

      AI designs computer chips in 6 hours. Humans take months. The students are teaching the teachers.

      New math

      Gemini’s Deep Think solved 4 previously-open mathematical problems during its evaluation. A first.

      And us?

      AI in our kitchens and laundry rooms.

      • Predictive maintenance — appliances that tell you they’ll need a service before they break.
      • Computer-vision cooking — ovens that recognize the dish and pick the program.
      • Connected learning — the washer that optimizes cycles based on how you actually use it.

      The same thread that started with Turing now runs through our product lines.

      Outro

      So… what’s next?

      Some open questions for the team.

      • 01 If an AI reasons better than us on a task, who is accountable when it gets it wrong — the engineer, the company, or the model?
      • 02 Where is the line between an appliance that helps you and one that decides for you — and who should draw it?
      • 03 As AI agents start doing multi-step work autonomously, which parts of our jobs should stay human — and why?
      • 04 Models are trained on data we all produced. Who owns the intelligence that comes out the other side?
      • 05 If the next 10 years of AI compress into 2, what’s the one thing we should start building at Electrolux today?
